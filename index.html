Subtractive Synthesis

  • Synthesisers shape electronic signals which, when you plug them into loudspeakers, make different kinds of noise
  • I've hooked my synthesiser up to an oscilloscope
  • Plugin to show you the shape of the sound they make
  • Sine waves sound soft
  • Sawtooth waves sound sharp
  • Square waves sound like a mix of both
  • Pulse waves can vary their pulse width to sound sharper or squarer
  • Changing the shape changes the texture of the sound
  • Synthesisers like the 106 and the 909 were old tech in the 90s
  • But they did have a new technology that would change electronic music forever
  • ...and that technology...
  • ...was MIDI
  • This is what MIDI cables and ports look like
  • Nearly all synthesisers have them now
  • MIDI cables are cheap and very easy to use

MIDI inputs

  • MIDI lets musicians synchronise machines of different types and brands
  • You could prepare performances at home on a sequencer
  • and mix them in a live performance on your midi-enabled synthesiser and drum machine
  • Synthesisers were a common sight in prog rock bands in the 70s
  • Slowly they went from being a part in a band...

Arturia KeyStep Pro

  • Let's hook this up to our browser using the Web MIDI API
  • Then we can see what MIDI data looks like.
  • MIDI sends a lot of data, but the essential core is a set of three values
  • The first value is the status byte, often used to identify the control
  • Some controls have different values depending on context, such as keyup or keydown
  • The second is the data byte, which carries information such as the note pressed
  • Additional parameters follow, such as the velocity or the range of a knob
  • You don't need API documentation to use these values, and weirdly it makes them easier to understand
  • They are designed to be learned intuitively, and professional audio software works the same way

Vocoder

  • The most characteristic sound in the song is the robotic voice
  • This is the instrument that Daft Punk are most associated with
  • The vocoder
  • A vocoder is a voice controller that allows you to control the amplication of a synthesiser using your voice
  • It measures the sound of your voice at a range of frequencies, and uses that information to filter a synthesised noise source
  • Vocoders can be either dedicated instruments or be a feature of a complete synthesiser, as in the case of this Microkorg

  • Dave Smith was an engineer and founder of Sequential
  • Creator of the Prophet 5, a famous polyphonic synthesiser
  • Together with the president of Roland, Ikutaro Kakehashi, they created MIDI
  • To this day, MIDI remains unlicensed. No fees have ever been collected.
  • This has led to its widespread adoption and near-universal compatibility
  • Whole industries and artforms exist because of MIDI, and it remains easy to use for beginners and experts alike
